Islam Stories — Dramatic Accuracy Protocol
The rules that govern what can and cannot appear in a script.
READ BY: every script generator call, every outline generator call.

THE CORE PRINCIPLE
Nothing appears in an Islam Stories script unless it traces
to a primary source. When sources disagree, both appear.
When sources are silent, we say so. When we dramatize,
we flag it as dramatization. The audience always knows
what is documented and what is interpreted.

This is not a constraint — it is the competitive advantage.
No other channel does this. It is why Islam Stories exists.


THE FIVE RULES

RULE 1: EVERY CLAIM HAS A SOURCE
Every specific historical claim in the script must be
attributed to a named primary source inline.

CORRECT:
  "Al-Tabari records that Khalid broke nine swords at Mu'tah."
  "According to Ibn Sa'd, Umar said..."
  "Al-Baladhuri writes that the army numbered..."

INCORRECT:
  "Sources say..."
  "Historians believe..."
  "It is widely accepted that..."
  "Legend has it..."

Name the source. Always.


RULE 2: CONFLICTS ARE DRAMA, NOT PROBLEMS
When primary sources disagree, both accounts appear
in the script. The disagreement is presented as
narrative tension — not resolved, not smoothed over.

TEMPLATE:
  "Al-Tabari records [X]. However, Al-Baladhuri gives
  a different account: [Y]. The sources do not agree —
  and that disagreement tells us something about how
  this moment was remembered."

The audience sits with the ambiguity.
That is what makes the series honest.


RULE 3: GAPS ARE STATED, NEVER FILLED
When the historical record is silent on a detail,
the script says so explicitly.

CORRECT:
  "History is silent on what Khalid said in that moment."
  "The sources do not record what happened next."
  "What he felt, we cannot know — the record only tells us
  what he did."

INCORRECT:
  Inventing dialogue to fill a gap.
  Inferring emotions not supported by any source.
  Creating dramatic moments with no historical basis.

"History does not record what happened next" is more
powerful than any invented detail. Use it.


RULE 4: CHAIN STRENGTH IS DISCLOSED
When citing hadith or transmitted accounts, note the
reliability of the chain of transmission.

LEVELS:
  Sahih (authenticated) — "In a sahih narration..."
  Hasan (good) — "In a well-attested narration..."
  Da'if (weak) — "In a narration whose chain is disputed..."
  Attributed — "Attributed to [figure], though the chain
                is not firmly established..."
  Scholarly consensus — "Most historians agree..."
  Singular — "In a narration reported only by [source],
              whose reliability is debated..."

The audience must know how solid the ground is
beneath each claim.


RULE 5: DRAMATIZATION IS FLAGGED
Creative latitude exists for Tier B and C figures.
But when dialogue, internal monologue, or scene-setting
goes beyond documented accounts, it must be flagged.

IN THE SCRIPT:
  [DRAMATIZED] — this dialogue is dramatic interpretation,
  not from primary sources. Grounded in documented
  motivations and character as established by [source].

IN THE NARRATION:
  "What words passed between them, we cannot know.
  But [source] tells us that the outcome was..."

The audience always knows the difference between
what is documented and what is imagined.


SENSITIVITY ACCURACY RULES

TIER S FIGURES (Prophet PBUH, Four Caliphs, Wives):
- No visual depiction of any kind
- No invented dialogue — EVER
- No description of physical appearance
- Authenticated quotes used in narration only, with citation
- Their presence felt through effects on others

TIER A FIGURES (Senior companions, Ahl al-Bayt):
- Authenticated quotes only — no dramatized dialogue
- All claims sourced inline
- Emotional scenes stay within documented accounts
- Karbala: ALWAYS both Sunni AND Shia accounts

TIER B FIGURES (All others):
- Full depiction permitted
- Dramatized dialogue allowed if flagged
- Internal monologue grounded in documented motivations
- Direct speech in quotes only for authenticated sayings

TIER C FIGURES (Contested):
- Document actions, never editorialize
- Acknowledge scholarly dispute explicitly
- Present their stated reasoning
- Never portray as simple villain


THE FOUR-SOURCE CHECK
Before any specific claim appears in a script:

1. Which primary source documents this?
2. What is the chain strength?
3. Do other sources agree or disagree?
4. If they disagree — is the disagreement surfaced?

If no primary source exists: the claim does not appear.
If the gap exists: "History does not record..."


THE KARBALA PROTOCOL
Special accuracy rules for the most sensitive event:
- Never take a position on legitimacy
- Present both Sunni AND Shia accounts
- Flag which account comes from which tradition
- Husayn never depicted directly
- Story told through Zaynab's perspective
- The 72: documented names, not anonymous masses
- Husayn's last words: cited with chain strength


THE SCHOLARLY DEBATE PROTOCOL
When modern scholars disagree on interpretation:
- Name the scholars and their positions
- Present the strongest version of each argument
- Do not resolve the debate
- Let the audience sit with the tension
- "Professor X argues [position]. However, Professor Y
  contends [counter-position]. The evidence supports
  elements of both readings."


WHAT EVERY EPISODE MUST DO
1. Name actual primary sources used (not "sources say")
2. Surface at least one scholarly debate or source conflict
3. Acknowledge at least one historical gap honestly
4. Flag all dramatized dialogue as interpretation
5. End on the question, not the answer
6. Include at least one moment where the sources disagree
   and the audience must decide for themselves


WHAT EVERY EPISODE MUST NOT DO
1. Invent facts not in any primary source
2. Resolve scholarly debates the sources leave open
3. Smooth over source conflicts for cleaner narrative
4. Use generic attribution ("it is said", "legend has it")
5. Depict Tier S figures in any way
6. Add invented dialogue for Tier S or A figures
7. End with moral certainty — ambiguity is the point
8. Use Western colonial framing as default perspective
