# Islam Stories — Creative Vision Document
## The Locked Concept

---

## The Logline

A cinematic shared universe of Islamic civilizational history —
each major figure gets their own character series, each era gets
its own world, and the crossovers are historically real.

---

## What This Actually Is

Not a documentary channel.
Not a history explainer channel.
Not a children's Islamic education channel.

A **character-driven cinematic universe** — built on YouTube,
structured like the Hellaverse, sourced like a scholarly journal,
and visually unlike anything in the history or Islamic content space.

Every episode ends with something unresolved.
Every character arc builds toward a convergence.
The crossovers aren't invented — they're in Al-Tabari.

---

## The Three-Layer Structure

### Layer 1: Character Series
Each major figure gets their own arc. 3–6 episodes. Standalone
but threaded to others. The audience invests in a person before
they invest in an era.

Examples:
- **Khalid ibn Walid** — The Sword of God (4 episodes)
  Ep1: The convert who fought hardest against Islam
  Ep2: The general who never lost
  Ep3: Yarmouk — the greatest victory
  Ep4: The dismissal — what happens to a weapon when the hand lets go

- **Nusayba bint Ka'ab** — She of the Two Belts (3 episodes)
  Ep1: The Pledge at Aqaba — two women among 73 men
  Ep2: Uhud — 12 wounds, the Prophet's words
  Ep3: Yamama — her sons, her hand, the end

- **Bilal ibn Rabah** — The Voice (3 episodes)
  Ep1: The slave who would not say it
  Ep2: Freed — but what does freedom mean now
  Ep3: The first adhan — and why he wept

- **Husayn ibn Ali** — The Refusal (4 episodes)
  Ep1: What Yazid's accession means
  Ep2: The road to Kufa — the letters, the promises
  Ep3: Karbala — told through Zaynab's eyes
  Ep4: Zaynab before Yazid — the speech that preserved it

- **Baybars** — The Slave Who Stopped the Mongols (4 episodes)
  Ep1: The slave market — who he was before
  Ep2: Ain Jalut — the battle that shouldn't have been won
  Ep3: The night ride to Damascus — and what he did on the way
  Ep4: Sultan — what power costs

- **Saladin** — The Mercy of Jerusalem (4 episodes)
  Ep1: The boy from Tikrit — unlikely path to power
  Ep2: Hattin — the battle that opened Jerusalem
  Ep3: Jerusalem — the mercy that shocked the Crusaders
  Ep4: Richard — mutual respect between enemies

- **Hasan al-Basri** — The Scholar Who Refused (3 episodes)
  Ep1: Grew up in the Prophet's household — saw everything
  Ep2: The civil wars — Siffin, Karbala, the aftermath
  Ep3: The withdrawal — chose conscience over court

- **Said ibn Jubayr** — The Last Words (2 episodes)
  Ep1: The scholar and the tyrant — years of pressure
  Ep2: The refusal — what he said before Al-Hajjaj killed him

- **Ibn Battuta** — The World Was Smaller Than He Thought (5 episodes)
  Ep1–4: Each episode one world region — Mali, India, Constantinople, China
  Ep5: The return — what 30 years of travel taught him
  [Note: Ibn Battuta is the crossover vehicle —
   he walks through finished stories and sees their aftermath]

- **Usman dan Fodio** — The Scholar Becomes a Commander (3 episodes)
  Ep1: 20 years of writing — the treatises
  Ep2: The jihad — when words weren't enough
  Ep3: Nana Asma'u — his daughter builds what he started

### Layer 2: Crossover Events
Major battles and turning points where multiple character series
converge. The audience arrives already invested in the people.
These are the payoff episodes.

**Battle of Yarmouk (636 CE)**
Converges: Khalid series + Abu Ubayda series
The two greatest generals of the early Islamic state on the
same battlefield. The audience has spent hours with both.
What does it mean when the best weapon and the most trusted
man serve together — and one of them is about to be dismissed?

**Karbala (680 CE)**
Converges: Husayn series + Zaynab series
Never depicted directly — told through Zaynab's eyes.
The secondary betrayal (Kufa abandons Husayn) runs beneath
the primary betrayal (Yazid's army). Two betrayals, one event.

**Battle of Ain Jalut (1260 CE)**
Converges: Baybars series + Qutuz story
The battle that stopped the Mongols. The night ride home.
The moment Baybars calculates that Qutuz won't give him
Aleppo. The killing. The sultanate. All in one episode.

**Jerusalem 1187 CE**
Converges: Saladin series + Richard series (from different sides)
The surrender terms. The mercy. The contrast with 1099.
The moment that defined how two civilizations see each other.

**The Yamama Convergence (632 CE)**
Converges: Nusayba series + Khalid series + Wahshi story
Khalid commands the army. Nusayba loses her hand. Wahshi
kills Musaylima — redemption for killing Hamza. Three
character arcs end in the same battle.

**Fall of Baghdad (1258 CE)**
Converges: Abbasid scholars series + Mongol arc
The end of the Abbasid caliphate. 800,000 killed. The
Tigris ran black with ink. The House of Wisdom destroyed.
What does a civilization feel when its center collapses?

---

### Layer 3: Era World-Building Episodes
Short connective episodes (8–10 min) that show what the world
looked like between the character arcs. Not about individuals —
about the texture of the civilization.

"What did the souks of 9th century Baghdad smell like?"
"What did a scholar's day look like in Cordoba?"
"What did an ordinary soldier eat before Yarmouk?"

These serve two purposes:
1. They deepen the world between major episodes
2. They're the easiest to produce — atmospheric, landscape-heavy,
   Fall of Civilizations register, minimal character animation

## The Visual Grammar — Locked

**One style. No switching.**

The painterly dark expressionist register — Persian miniature
as color DNA, Arcane as character scene benchmark,
Puss in Boots Last Wish as battle action register,
Kings and Generals as tactical map reference.

**Five scene types — all within this single style:**

| Scene Type | Register | When |
|---|---|---|
| Establishing | Fall of Civilizations | Geography, cities, opening landscape |
| Tactical Map | Kings and Generals — parchment palette | Battle overview |
| Character/Drama | Arcane — single light source, expressionist | Confrontations, decisions |
| Battle/Action | Puss in Boots Last Wish — ink-wash, speed lines | Combat sequences |
| Manuscript | Islamic illuminated manuscript | Primary source citations |

**The unifying palette:** warm amber → deep shadow → jewel tones.
No clean digital edges. No photorealistic rendering. No modern lighting.

## The Sensitivity Architecture

**Built into every episode from the ground up:**

Tier S (Prophet PBUH, Four Caliphs, wives) — never depicted.
Referenced only through others' eyes. No invented dialogue ever.

Tier A (Husayn, Zaynab, Hamza, Fatimah, senior companions) —
depicted through witnesses. Karbala is Zaynab's story, not
Husayn's. We never see his face, only her account.

Tier B (warriors, scholars, converts, later figures) —
full cinematic depiction. This is where the drama lives.

Tier C (Yazid, Aurangzeb, Al-Hajjaj) — documented actions only.
The drama is in the complexity, not the judgment.

## The Audience

**Primary:**
- Muslim diaspora adults 25–45 — know the names, not the depth
- History YouTube regulars 18–40 — actively looking for this gap
- Adult animation audience 16–35 — drawn by visual register

**The crossover model builds a third audience:**
People who start with one character series (Khalid, say) and
stay for the universe. Like fans who watched Helluva Boss
before they knew about Hazbin Hotel. The universe rewards
binging in a way a documentary channel never can.

## The Production Model

**Hazbin Hotel / indie animation path — not Fall of Civilizations:**

Phase 1: Pilot episode — "The Sword of God" (Khalid ibn Walid Ep1)
Patreon-funded, freelance animators, YouTube-first.
The pilot proves the concept and builds the audience.

Phase 2: First character arc complete (4 episodes)
Khalid ibn Walid full arc. Establishes the visual universe.
If the arc performs — launch second character series in parallel.

Phase 3: First crossover event
Battle of Yarmouk — Khalid + Abu Ubayda convergence.
This is the episode that proves the shared universe concept.
Fan theories, "wait that's the same timeline" moments,
rewatch value goes through the roof.

Phase 4: Two character series running simultaneously
Different eras, different visual palettes within the style.
Rashidun series and Scholars series in parallel.
Audience picks their entry point, discovers the other.

**The streaming deal comes to you — you don't pitch it.**
That's the Hazbin Hotel lesson. 114 million views on a
Patreon-funded pilot. A24 called them.

## The Knowledge Layer's Role

Every character arc, every crossover event, every crossover
moment is already sourced in the DB.

The orchestrator retrieves:
- Primary accounts (Al-Tabari, Ibn Sa'd, Al-Baladhuri)
- Character motivations (why they actually made that choice)
- Source conflicts (where historians disagree — the drama)
- Scholarly debates (what the script must acknowledge)
- Death records (last words, witnesses, significance)
- Quotes (authenticated sayings with chain strength)

The scripts are grounded. Every claim cites a source.
Every conflict is surfaced. Every gap is acknowledged.

The knowledge layer is what makes this different from
every other Islamic history channel — it's not someone's
interpretation. It's the authenticated record, dramatized.
